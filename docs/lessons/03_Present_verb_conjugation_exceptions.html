Present Tense Verb Conjugation - Exceptions

Author

Will Gammerdinger

Published

Invalid Date

Approximate time: 45 minutes

Learning Objectives

In this lesson, the learner will:

  • Categorize exceptions to the “normal” way of conjugating verbs in the present tense

Sein

Sein (to be) is probably the most difficult verb to conjugate in the present tense because it doesn’t follow any particular rules and you need to just memorize its conjugation. The good news is that you will you use it so frequently that it will become second-nature rather quickly. Below is the conjugation table for sein:

ich bin wir sind
du bist ihr seid
er ist
sie ist
es ist		 Sie sind
sie sind

Infinitive stem endings that cause issues

There are some sets of verbs where the verb stem (the verb after removing -en) ends in letters that require special care. We will talk about these in the sections below.

Infinitive stems ending in -s, -ß, -x or -z

Infinitive verbs whose stem ends in -s, -ß, -x or -z only add a -t in the second-person singular. The rationale for this is that these infinitive stems already have either an -s sound at the end of the stem or a sound that is somewhat similar to -s. As a result, adding an -st ending wouldn’t be heard any different than -t. Some common words that fall into this category are:

-s -ß -x -z
reisen (to travel) heißen (to be named) faxen (to fax) tanzen (to dance)
schließen (to close) sitzen (to sit)
grüßen (to greet) besitzen (to own)
NoteRelation between -ß and -ss

This is probably a good place to point out that -s and -ß related. The -ß is sometimes replaced with -ss. As such, rules that apply to -s will also apply to -ß.

Let’s see an example of one of these conjugations:

ich heiße wir heißen
du heißt ihr heißt
er heißt
sie heißt
es heißt		 Sie heißen
sie heißen

Infinitive stems ending in -d or -t

Infinitive stems ending in -d or -t need to have an -e added after the infinitive stem but prior to the -st or -t in order to be able to pronounce it. Some common words that fall into this category are:

-d -t
baden (to bathe) arbeiten (to work)
schneiden (to cut) antworten (to answer)
laden (to load or to charge) retten (to rescue)
beten (to pray)
Note

This rule is a pretty consistent rule, but there are a handful of exceptions. Some of these exceptions included:

  • geraten - to get into/to fall into
  • raten - to advise
  • treten - to tread/to kick

These verbs aren’t super common for beginners to come across, so you can think of this rule as pretty steadfast at this moment, but if you want to have it in the back of your mind that a few odd exceptions exist, then that is probably more than good enough.

Infinitive stems ending in -m or -n preceded by a consonant sound other than -l or -r

Additionally, if the verb stem ends in an -m or -n that is preceded by a consonant sound other than -l or -r then we need to add an -e, just like the circumstances of infinitive stems ending with -d or -t above.

This rule can be a bit difficult for learners because it matters how one defines a “consonant sound”. Does the sound before the -m or -n sound like a vowel, -l or r? If so, it is treated normally. Otherwise, we need to add an -e. Let’s look at common application of this rule, then look at examples where we need to think about this “consonant sound” idea.

atmen (to breathe)

ich atme wir atmen
du atmest ihr atmet
er atmet
sie atmet
es atmet		 Sie atmen
sie atmen

öffnen (to open)

ich öffne wir öffnen
du öffnest ihr öffnet
er öffnet
sie öffnet
es öffnet		 Sie öffnen
sie öffnen

A table of common verbs that fall into the category of requiring an -e to be added are below:

-m preceded by a consonant sound other than -l or -r -n preceded by a consonant sound other than -l or -r
atmen (to breathe) öffnen (to open)
rechnen (to calculate)
zeichnen (to draw)
trocknen (to dry (something))
NoteComment on the frequency of this category of verbs

There aren’t a ton of verbs that fall into this category. In fact, for the category of having an -m preceded by a consonant sound other than -l or -r, atmen, and it’s derivatives (einatmen (to breathe in), ausatmen (to breathe out), durchatmen (to catch your breath), aufatmen (to breathe a sigh of relief), etc.) is the only verb (that I am aware of) that falls into this category.

For infinitive stems with -n preceded by a consonant sound other than -l or -r, it is also quite limited. The table above covers all of the most common ones and a handful of others do exist (begegnen (to meet (by chance)), entgegnen (to reply (often contradictory)), segnen (to bless), etc.), but they are quite infrequent to come across as a beginner.

NoteInfinitive stems ending in an -m or -n that are preceded by vowel sounds, -l or -r do not add an -e to the infinitive stem

To prove the rule, let’s look at a few infinitive stems that either end in -m or -n and are preceded by a vowel sound, -l or -r.

lernen (to learn)

ich lerne wir lernen
du lernst ihr lernt
er lernt
sie lernt
es lernt		 Sie lernen
sie lernen

wärmen (to warm)

ich wärme wir wärmen
du wärmst ihr wärmt
er wärmt
sie wärmt
es wärmt		 Sie wärmen
sie wärmen

Unlike the infinitive stems ending in -m or -n preceded by a consonant sound other than l or r, these are relatively more common. I have provided a list below of some of the more common ones you might come across:

  • beginnen (to begin)
  • sich besinnen (to recollect/to think better of)
  • brennen (to burn)
  • dienen (to serve (a cause))
  • entfernen (to remove (something))
  • erwähnen (to mention)
  • filmen (to film)
  • sich gewöhnen* (to become accustomed)
  • kennenlernen (to get to know/to meet)
  • kommen (to come)
  • lernen (to learn)
  • sich lohnen* (to be worth it)
  • meinen (to mean)
  • wärmen (to warm)
  • wohnen* (to reside (somewhere))

* Note in these examples that the -n is preceded by an -h and you may be inclined to think that these are breaking the rule. However, in this context, the sound prior to the -n still has a “vowel-like” sound. This is part of the reason why this rule is the tricky.

I am also not aware of any circumstances where the infinitive stem has a -ln pattern and filmen is the only one I know with the -lm pattern.

Infinitives ending in -n rather than -en

Most verbs in German end in -en, but there are a handful that rather end with just an -n. In order to conjugate these verbs, treat the -n as if it were -en, by removing it and adding the normal endings. Some examples from this group of verbs are:

  • handeln (to trade)
  • sammeln (to collect)
  • lächeln (to smile)
  • segeln (to sail)
  • wechseln (to change or to exchange)
  • erinnern (to remember)
  • ändern (to change)

We can use segeln as an example to see how to conjugate these verbs:

ich segele wir segelen
du segelst ihr segelt
er segelt
sie segelt
es segelt		 Sie segelen
sie segelen
NoteA consequence of most of these verbs ending in -eln and -ern

One small thing to note about these verbs is they all end in -ern or -eln, so you don’t need to worry about the above circumstances where the verb has a stem that ends in -d, -m, -n, -s, -t, -ß, -x or -z once you remove the -n.

NoteExcluding sein tun from this category

One could argue that sein (to be) tun (to do) also end in -n rather than -en, but since they are both irregular verbs, so we will handle them elsewhere.

Impersonal Verbs

There are a handful of verbs that really only exist in the third-person. All of them deal either defining the state of something or are weather-related. People are unable to do these verbs and, therefore, they are referred to as impersonal verbs. Some examples are in the tabset below:

  • Define the state of something
  • Weather-related verbs

Some impersonal verbs which define the status of a thing or an event are:

  • stattfinden (to happen/to take place)
  • passieren (to happen)
  • eilen (to be urgent)
  • stimmen (to be true)
  • sich lohnen (to be worth it)

Other impersonal verbs fall within the category of being weather-related, such as:

  • hageln (to hail)
  • regnen (to rain)
  • schneien (to snow)
  • blitzen* (to lightning)
  • donnern* (to thunder)

* Note that these verbs in the context of weather, only have third-person conjugations, but they do have alternative meanings that can be conjugated in the first- and second- person. See the collapsible discussion below for more information.

As you can see, these are all verbs that people can’t “do”. You can’t rain, I can’t be true and we can’t happen, but it can hail, be true or happen. These impersonal verbs exist in third-person singular and plural.

You might wonder how the weather-related impersonal verbs can occur in the third-person plural. When they are used in the weather-related context, they are usually referring to the things that are coming from the sky, the hailstones, raindrops, snowflakes, etcetera. For example:

  • Die Hagelkörner hageln gegen das Fenster. - The hailstones are hailing against the window.
  • Flocken schneien vom Himmel. - The snowflakes are snowing from the sky.
  • Regenschauer regnen auf die Stadt nieder. - Rain showers are raining down on the city.

However, in most instances you’ll run into these weather-related impersonal verbs in the third-person singular:

  • Es hagelt. - It is hailing.
  • Es schneit. - It is snowing.
  • Es regnet - It is raining
NotePrefixes with schneien, which change the meaning

There are separable prefixes that can be added to schneien (rein‑ / herein‑ / vorbei‑), which change the meaning from “to snow” to “to stop by (casually/unexpectedly)”. As a result, these can exist in first- and second-person and you may seen them in a sentence like:

  • Ich schneie später rein. - I am stopping by later.
NoteAdditional definitions for blitzen and donnern

Since blitzen (to lightning) and donnern (to thunder) are weather-related verbs, you may be tempted to not concern yourself with them in the first- and second-person. And in the context of weather-related events, they do act as impersonal verbs since we can’t lightning or you can’t thunder (in the weather sense). However, each of these verbs have a secondary definition that can be used in the first- and second-person without any prefixes (unlike schneien; see the collapsible discussion above). For blitzen, it can refer to flashing one’s eyes. For donnern, like English, it can also mean to make loud noises. The takeaway message is that not all weather-related verbs fail to have first- and second-person conjugations. But it is true that these weather-related verbs act as impersonal verbs when discussing weather. However, they aren’t impersonal verbs for other definitions.

- **handeln** (to trade)
- **sammeln** (to collect)
- **lächeln** (to smile)
- **segeln** (to sail)
- **wechseln** (to change or to exchange)
- **erinnern** (to remember)
- **ändern** (to change)

# Impersonal Verbs

There are a handful of verbs that really only exist in the third-person. All of them deal either defining the state of something or are weather-related. People are unable to *do* these verbs and, therefore, they are referred to as ***impersonal verbs***. Some examples are in the tabset below:

:::{.panel-tabset}
## Define the state of something

Some impersonal verbs which define the status of a thing or an event are:

- **stattfinden** (to happen/to take place)
- **passieren** (to happen)
- **eilen** (to be urgent)
- **stimmen** (to be true)
- **sich lohnen** (to be worth it)

## Weather-related verbs

Other impersonal verbs fall within the category of being weather-related, such as:

- **hageln** (to hail)
- **regnen** (to rain)
- **schneien** (to snow)
- **blitzen\*** (to lightning)
- **donnern\*** (to thunder)

\* *Note that these verbs in the context of weather, only have third-person conjugations, but they do have alternative meanings that can be conjugated in the first- and second- person. See the collapsible discussion below for more information.*

:::

As you can see, these are all verbs that people can't "do". *You* can't rain, *I* can't be true and *we* can't happen, but *it* can hail, be true or happen. These impersonal verbs exist in third-person singular and plural.


You might wonder how the weather-related impersonal verbs can occur in the third-person plural. When they are used in the weather-related context, they are usually referring to the things that are coming from the sky, the hailstones, raindrops, snowflakes, etcetera. For example:

- **Die Hagelkörner hageln gegen das Fenster.** - *The hailstones are hailing against the window.*
- **Flocken schneien vom Himmel.** - *The snowflakes are snowing from the sky.*
- **Regenschauer regnen auf die Stadt nieder.** - *Rain showers are raining down on the city.*

However, in most instances you'll run into these weather-related impersonal verbs in the third-person singular:

- **Es hagelt.** - *It is hailing.*
- **Es schneit.** - *It is snowing.*
- **Es regnet** - *It is raining*

::: {.callout-note collapse="true"}
## Prefixes with *schneien*, which change the meaning

There are separable prefixes that can be added to **schneien** (rein‑ / herein‑ / vorbei‑), which change the meaning from "to snow" to "to stop by (casually/unexpectedly)". As a result, these can exist in first- and second-person and you may seen them in a sentence like:

- **Ich schneie später rein.** - *I am stopping by later.*

:::

:::{.callout-note collapse="true"}
## Additional definitions for *blitzen* and *donnern*

Since **blitzen** (to lightning) and **donnern** (to thunder) are weather-related verbs, you may be tempted to not concern yourself with them in the first- and second-person. And in the context of weather-related events, they do act as impersonal verbs since *we* can't lightning or *you* can't thunder (in the weather sense). However, each of these verbs have a secondary definition that can be used in the first- and second-person without any prefixes (unlike schneien; *see the collapsible discussion above*). For **blitzen**, it can refer to flashing one's eyes. For **donnern**, like English, it can also mean to make loud noises. The takeaway message is that *not all* weather-related verbs fail to have first- and second-person conjugations. But it is true that these weather-related verbs act as impersonal verbs when discussing weather. However, they aren't impersonal verbs for other definitions. 

:::
