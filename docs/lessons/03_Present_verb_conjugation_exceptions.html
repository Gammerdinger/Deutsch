Sein

Sein mean “to be” and it is probably the most difficult verb to conjugate in the present tense because it doesn’t follow any particular rules and you need to just memorize its conjugation. The good news is that you will you use it so frequently that it will become second-nature rather quickly. Below is the conjugation table for Sein:

ich bin wir sind
du bist ihr seid
er ist
sie ist
es ist		 Sie sind
sie sind

Infinitive stems ending in -s, -ß, -x or -z

Infinitive verbs whose stem ends in -s, -ß, -x or -z has only uses -t in the for the second-person singular. The rationale I use for this is that these infinitive stems already have either an -s sound at the end of the stem or a sound that is similar to -s. As a result adding an -st ending wouldn’t be heard any different than -t. Some common words that fall into this category are:

-s -ß -x -z
reisen (to travel) heißen (to be named) faxen (to fax) tanzen (to dance)
schließen (to close) sitzen (to sit)
grüßen (to greet) besitzen (to own)

This is probably a good place to point out that -s and -ß related. The -ß is sometimes replaced with -ss. As such, rules that apply to -s, will also apply to -ß.

Let’s see an example of one of these conjugations:

ich heiße wir heißen
du heißt ihr heißt
er heißt
sie heißt
es heißt		 Sie heißen
sie heißen

Infinitive stems ending in -d, -t or if it ends in an -m or -n that is not preceded by a consonant other than l or r

Infinitive stems ending in -d or -t need to have an e added after the infinitive stem but prior to the -st or -t in order to be able to pronounce it. However, if it ends in an -m or -n that is not preceded by a consonant other than l or an r then we add an e as well. A table of common verbs that fall into this category are below:

-d -t -m not preceded by r -n not preceded by r
baden (to bathe) arbeiten (to work) atmen (to breathe) öffnen (to open)
schneiden (to cut) antworten (to answer) rechnen (to calculate)
laden (to load or to charge) retten (to rescue) zeichnen (to draw)
beten (to pray) regnen (to rain)

Infinitive stems ending in -n rather than -en

Most verbs in German end in -en, but there are a handful that rather just end with an -n. In order to conjugate these verbs, treat the -n as if it were -en, by removing it and adding the normal endings. Some examples from this group of verbs are:

  • handeln (to trade)
  • sammeln (to collect)
  • lächeln (to smile)
  • segeln (to sail)
  • wechseln (to change or to exchange)
  • erinnern (to remember)
  • ändern (to change)
  • tun (to do)

We can use segeln as an example to see how to conjugate these verbs:

ich segele wir segelen
du segelst ihr segelt
er segelt
sie segelt
es segelt		 Sie segelen
sie segelen
TipConsequences of most of these verbs end in -eln and -ern

One small thing to note about these verbs is they almost end in -ern or -eln (with the exception of tun (to do)), so you don’t need to worry about the above circumstances where the verb has a stem that ends in -d, -m, -n, -s, -t, -ß, -x or -z once you remove the -n.

TipExcluding sein from this category

One could argue that sein (to be) also ends in -n rather than -en, but since it is different from every other verb in German and doesn’t follow these patterns, we are going to categorize it on its own elsewhere.

Stem-changing verbs

Some verbs change their stem in the second-person singular and the third-person singular. Unfortunately, there aren’t any tricks or mnemonics for recognizing which ones do stem change, which ones don’t. However, the good news is that many are used frequently enough that you will likely learn the most common offenders quickly. There are three classes of stem-changing verbs:

  • a → ä
  • e → i
  • e → ie

Irregular conjugations

In addition to sein, there are a handful of irregular verbs that don’t fall neatly into any of the above categories. Many of these are used frequently, so you will likely learn that they are different rather quickly. A brief list of the most common ones are:

  • dürfen (to allow/to permit/may)
  • gebären (to give birth to)
  • gelten (to be valid/to be regarded)
  • geraten (to get into/to fall into) and related raten (to advise)
  • haben (to have)
  • halten (to hold/to maintain a state/to stop)
  • können (to be able to/can)
  • mögen (to like)
  • müssen (to have to/must)
  • nehmen (to take)
  • sein (to be)
  • sollen (is supposed to be/is allegedly)
  • werden (to become)
  • wissen (to know, as in facts)

Impersonal Verbs

There are a handful of verbs that really only exist in the third-person. All of them deal either defining the state of something or are weather-related. People are unable to do these verbs and, therefore, they are referred to as impersonal verbs. Some examples are in the tabset below:

  • Define the state of something
  • Weather-related verbs

Some impersonal verbs which define the status of a thing or an event are:

  • stattfinden (to happen/to take place)
  • passiern (to happen)
  • eilen (to be urgent)
  • stimmen (to be true)
  • sich lohnen (to be worth it)

Other impersonal verbs fall within the category of being weather-related, such as:

  • hageln (to hail)
  • regnen (to rain)
  • schneien (to snow)
  • blitzen* (to lightning)
  • donnern* (to thunder)

* Note that these verbs in the context of weather, only have third-person conjugations, but they do have alternative meanings that can be conjugated in the first- and second- person. See the collapsible discussion below for more information.

As you can see, these are all verbs that people can’t “do”. You can’t rain, I can’t be true and we can’t happen, but it can hail, be true or happen. These impersonal verbs exist in third-person singular and plural.

You might wonder how the weather-related impersonal verbs can occur in the third-person plural. When they are used in the weather-related context, they are usually referring to the things that are coming from the sky, the hailstones, raindrops, snowflakes, etcetera. For example:

  • Die Hagelkörner hageln gegen das Fenster. - The hailstones are hailing against the window.
  • Flocken schneien vom Himmel. - The snowflakes are snowing from the sky.
  • Regenschauer regnen auf die Stadt nieder. - Rain showers are raining down on the city.

However, in most instances you’ll run into these weather-related impersonal verbs in the third-person singular:

  • Es hagelt. - It is hailing.
  • Es schneit. - It is snowing.
  • Es regnet - It is raining
NotePrefixes with schneien, which change the meaning

There are separable prefixes that can be added to schneien (rein‑ / herein‑ / vorbei‑), which change the meaning from “to snow” to “to stop by (casually/unexpectedly)”. As a result, these can exist in first- and second-person and you may seen them in a sentence like:

  • Ich schneie später rein. - I am stopping by later.
NoteAdditional definitions for blitzen and donnern

Since blitzen (to lightning) and donnern (to thunder) are weather-related verbs, you may be tempted to not concern yourself with them in the first- and second-person. And in the context of weather-related events, they do act as impersonal verbs since we can’t lightning or you can’t thunder (in the weather sense). However, each of these verbs have a secondary definition that can be used in the first- and second-person without any prefixes (unlike schneien; see the collapsible discussion above). For blitzen, it can refer to flashing one’s eyes. For donnern, like English, it can also mean to make loud noises. The takeaway message is that not all weather-related verbs fail to have first- and second-person conjugations. But it is true that these weather-related verbs act as impersonal verbs when discussing weather. However, they aren’t impersonal verbs for other definitions.
