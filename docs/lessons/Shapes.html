Counting corners for 2-dimensional shapes

Polygons

Polygons in German are a little easier than in English, but it generally follows a similar pattern. As you increase the number of sides in English after a quadrilateral, you simply have a numerical prefix (penta-, hexa-, etc.) followed by -gon. The etymology of -gon in reference to shapes traces back to the Greek suffix -gōnia (-γωνο), which means angles. So something like pentagon can be broken down into “five angles”/“five corners”.

In German, like in English, they count the corners, but instead of waiting until pentagon to adopt the numerical prefix + -corner convention, they start it at triangle. The German word for corner is generally die Ecke, but das Eck is another, less common way, to say corner. You may hear das Eck in Southern Germany, Austria and parts of Switzerland. It is also used to count the corners of polygons in German though. The table below to see the pattern for counting the size of a polygon in German:

English German English German
triangle das Dreieck quadrilateral das Viereck
pentagon das Fünfeck hexagon das Sechseck
octagon das Achteck decagon das Zehneck

We can see that it is simply the number of corners in the polygon, followed by -eck suffix. In fact, polygon translates directly to das Vieleck (viel is the German work for many), so the connections between German and English for polygons is pretty strong.

Special quadrilaterals

While this pattern works for generally describing polygons, there are also some special quadrilaterals in German as well. In the same way that squares, rectangles and parallelograms are all special quadrilaterals in English, there are special names for different quadrilaterals, which can be found in the table below:

English German
square das Quadrat
rectangle das Rechteck
parallelogram das Parallelogramm
rhombus / diamond die Raute
trapezoid das Trapez

Curved Shapes

The table below outlines the curved shapes:

English German
circle der Kreis
semicircle der Halbkreis*
oval das Oval

* You can see that having our knowledge of fractions can help here since Halbkreis literally translates to “half circle”.

Counting sides for 3-dimensional shapes

Prisms

Let’s start discussing three-dimensional shapes with the prisms, because they follow a pattern, but it isn’t likely the what you would guess given a background in English. In English, we take the name for the face of the prism and often add “-al prism” (pentagonal prism, hexagonal prism, etc.). Notable triangular and rectangular prisms don’t fit that pattern in English. The good news is that all prisms in German follow the same pattern, but for reason German counts the sides on the face shape rather than the corners for prisms. Of course the number of sides and corners are equal in polygons, so it doesn’t matter if you count sides or corners, but it is a mnemonic that helps be think about shapes in German.

The German word for “side” in relation to shapes is die Seite (notably also means page) and “-sided” is -seitig and “prism” is das Prisma. So let’s see how German constructs prism shapes in the table below:

English German
triangular prism das dreiseitige Prisma
quadrilateral prism das vierseitige Prisma
pentagonal prism das fünfseitige Prisma
hexagonal prism das sechseitige Prisma

You will notice that -e is appended to the end of number + seitig. This has to do with “three-sided”, “four-sided”, etc. are adjectives and this is the behavior for adjectives in this context. We are going to talk more about adjectives at a later point, so for now you can just remember to add -e.

Special shapes

Unfortunately like cube, cone and sphere in English, German has some unique names for some three-dimensional shapes that just need to be memorized. There is no pattern that you can rely on.

English German
cube der Würfel
rectangular prism der Quader*
triangular pyramid die dreiseitige Pyramide
square pyramid die vierseitige Pyramide
cylinder der Zylinder
cone der Kegel
sphere die Kugel
hemisphere die Halbkugel**

* A rectangular prism (der Quader) is a special type of quadrilateral prism (das vierseitiges Prisma), because a prism of a trapezoid would be also be a type of quadrilateral prism, but not a rectangular prism. When describing a rectangular prism most Germans would use der Quader over das vierseitiges Prisma in the same way that English speakers would like use rectangular prism over quadrilateral prism. It sounds a bit geometry textbook-ish to say das vierseitiges Prisma just like quadrilateral prism sounds in English.

** Once again, like Halbkreis, it is useful to know fractions since Halbkugel literally translates to “half sphere”.
